QCosmo

Jul 29, 2020

Quantum characterization and control of single molecules

Goal of the project

In the last decade, a worldwide effort to build a usable quantum computer has been made. Trapped atomic ions are one of the most promising physical quantum computing architectures and have been the focus of our research at the University of Innsbruck.

Within QCosmo, we aim to explore the physics and harness the computational potential of a more complex trapped ion system: polyatomic molecules. As a first task, we tackle the long standing challenge of preparing, controlling and characterizing single polyatomic molecules at the quantum level using techniques that have been developed for quantum computing with atomic ions.

Quantum logic with trapped molecular ions

Performing spectroscopy on molecular ions has a long history. Unfortunately, most experimental methods destroy the molecules in order to detect their state. Within this project we will use logic methods that have been invented to enable spectroscopy on inaccessible atomic ions that cannot be read-out with existing methods. These quantum logic methods couple the ion of interest to an atomic ion that is suitable for quantum computing and quantum control techniques have already been developed. Quantum logic powers one of today’s most precise atomic clocks.

The core concept of quantum logic spectroscopy is based on the coupling of the spectroscopy ion’s motion to the logic ion’s motion via the strong electromagnetic interaction. This coupling allows us to transfer the techniques that have been developed for quantum computing, onto less accessible species.

Lately, these techniques have been transferred to molecular ions at PTB, NIST, and the University of Basel. To date, these quantum logic methods have been limited to diatomic molecules and to precision spectroscopy at the micro- to millisecond timescale. We aim to transfer these techniques to ulatrafast timescales and to complex polyatomic molecules, opening a new window into investigating dynamic processes of molecular ions that are not well studied yet.

Characterizing ultrafast processes

Internal processes in molecules occur often at ultrafast picosecond and femtosecond timescales, which makes them hard to access by standard spectroscopic techniques. To overcome this problem, ultrafast time domain spectroscopy has been invented which uses a series of laser pulses at the femtosecond timescale to gain information about molecular processes. We plan to transfer these processes to trapped ion systems by exploiting the fact that each photon absorption event comes with a small kick to molecule. We will exploit a single photon absorption detection technique by measuring the recoil of absorbing a single photon using the co-trapped atomic ion. This technique is independent of the molecular species and the transition type and will thus provide a solid basis of the following experiments. The recoil from absorbing a molecule is instant and thus the experimentally accessible timescale is given by the lasers that interrogate the molecules.

More details on the proposed method can be found in the publication Ultrafast infrared spectroscopy with single molecular ions
